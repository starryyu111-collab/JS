\section{Results and Discussion}
\label{sec:results_discussion}

\subsection{Main Results}
Table~\ref{tab:main_results} summarizes the classification accuracy and quantization-error metrics of the FP32 reference, the INT8 MinMax sanity-check baseline, and the three INT4 PTQ variants. The FP32 model achieves 94.13\% Top-1 accuracy on the CIFAR-10 test set. INT8-MinMax gives 94.11\%, corresponding to only a 0.02 percentage-point drop, which indicates that the simulated quantization and evaluation pipeline is well behaved in the 8-bit setting.

The INT4-MinMax baseline suffers a clear accuracy degradation, dropping from 94.13\% to 88.20\%. This behavior is consistent with the expected sensitivity of low-bit activation quantization to large calibration-time activation values. When MinMax calibration assigns the full 4-bit activation range according to the maximum observed activation, rare large values can enlarge the quantization interval for the majority of post-ReLU activations.

Both clipping-based INT4 methods alleviate the accuracy degradation relative to INT4-MinMax. INT4-P99.9 improves Top-1 accuracy from 88.20\% to 92.97\%, while INT4-MSE-Selected improves it to 92.86\%. Therefore, the proposed method can be described as reducing the degradation of INT4 PTQ compared with MinMax calibration. However, in this run, INT4-MSE-Selected does not achieve the highest Top-1 accuracy among the INT4 methods: INT4-P99.9 is higher by 0.11 percentage points. For this reason, the discussion should emphasize reconstruction-error reduction and layer sensitivity rather than claiming that the proposed method is the best-accuracy INT4 method.

\subsection{Activation Histogram and Outlier Interpretation}
An activation histogram is useful for interpreting why MinMax calibration can be fragile in INT4 PTQ. For post-ReLU activations, the distribution is non-negative and may contain a small number of large values in the tail. In such a case, MinMax calibration preserves the largest observed value but assigns only 16 quantization levels to the entire interval from zero to that maximum. Consequently, the dense region near zero may be quantized with a coarse step size. Percentile clipping intentionally saturates part of the high-activation tail so that the available INT4 levels can better cover the activation range where most samples lie. \subsection{Activation and Logit Reconstruction Error}
Figure~\ref{fig:error_metrics} compares the activation reconstruction MSE and logit MSE of the quantized methods. The largest errors occur under INT4-MinMax, whose activation MSE is 0.00273660 and logit MSE is 1.84912006. After activation clipping, both error metrics decrease substantially. INT4-MSE-Selected reduces activation MSE to 0.00034846 and logit MSE to 0.41728575 compared with INT4-MinMax. This supports the interpretation that selecting layer-wise activation clipping thresholds can reduce the reconstruction error introduced by simulated INT4 activation quantization.

Compared with the fixed INT4-P99.9 baseline, INT4-MSE-Selected has slightly lower activation MSE and logit MSE in this run. Specifically, its activation MSE is 0.00034846 compared with 0.00035606 for INT4-P99.9, and its logit MSE is 0.41728575 compared with 0.46350010. However, this lower reconstruction error does not translate into the highest Top-1 accuracy, since INT4-P99.9 remains slightly better in accuracy. This suggests that lower activation or logit MSE is informative for diagnosing quantization behavior, but it is not a perfect substitute for classification accuracy.

\subsection{Layer-wise Activation MSE}
Figure~\ref{fig:layerwise_mse} reports the layer-wise activation reconstruction MSE under the MSE-selected clipping procedure. The purpose of this figure is not to introduce an additional ablation, but to show that quantization error is not uniform across activation sites. Some layers are more sensitive to the INT4 activation range than others, which motivates choosing clipping thresholds on a layer-by-layer basis rather than applying a single numerical threshold to all activations.

The threshold table generated by the MSE-selected calibration procedure records, for each observed post-ReLU activation module, the selected percentile, the corresponding clipping threshold, and the candidate reconstruction errors. These records support the interpretation that the proposed method performs candidate-based layer-wise selection rather than using a globally shared clipping value. Since the selection is restricted to the predefined percentile set, the result should be interpreted as MSE-selected within the candidate set, not as continuous-space global optimization.

\subsection{Discussion and Limitations}
The results show that activation clipping is important for simulated INT4 PTQ in this CIFAR-10 case study. Naive INT4-MinMax causes a 5.93 percentage-point accuracy drop, whereas the two clipping-based INT4 methods reduce the drop to around 1.2 percentage points. INT4-MSE-Selected also reduces activation reconstruction error and logit MSE compared with INT4-MinMax, and it slightly improves both error metrics compared with the fixed P99.9 baseline.

At the same time, the conclusions should be kept within the actual experimental scope. First, the evaluation uses one dataset, one model, and one recorded random seed. Second, no calibration-size sensitivity study or additional architecture is included. Third, the proposed method searches only a small candidate percentile set, so it should not be described as globally optimal clipping. Finally, all results are obtained with simulated PTQ / fake quantization. No real INT4 inference kernel, hardware latency, throughput, energy, or deployment speedup is measured. Therefore, this work should not claim state-of-the-art performance or real hardware acceleration.
