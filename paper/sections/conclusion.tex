\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

This paper presented a controlled study of activation clipping for simulated INT4 post-training quantization of a CIFAR-10 CNN classifier. Starting from a trained FP32 \texttt{resnet18\_cifar} model, we compared an INT8-MinMax sanity-check baseline, an INT4-MinMax baseline, a fixed-percentile INT4-P99.9 baseline, and the proposed INT4-MSE-Selected method. The proposed method selects the post-ReLU activation clipping threshold independently for each observed activation layer by minimizing reconstruction MSE over a predefined percentile candidate set during calibration. The procedure is performed after training, does not update model weights, and should be interpreted as simulated PTQ rather than quantization-aware training.

The experimental results show that naive INT4-MinMax calibration causes a large accuracy drop on CIFAR-10, while activation clipping substantially reduces this degradation. In the reported run, INT4-P99.9 and INT4-MSE-Selected both recover much of the accuracy lost by INT4-MinMax. INT4-MSE-Selected also reduces activation reconstruction MSE and logit MSE compared with INT4-MinMax, and the layer-wise MSE analysis shows non-uniform reconstruction-error behavior across activation layers. At the same time, the accuracy improvement of INT4-MSE-Selected is limited relative to the fixed P99.9 baseline; therefore, the main finding is that layer-wise MSE-selected clipping is useful for reducing reconstruction error and showing layer-dependent calibration behavior, rather than establishing a universally best INT4 accuracy setting.

This study has several limitations. First, all experiments are conducted only on CIFAR-10. Second, the evaluation uses only one compact CNN setting, namely the ResNet-18-CIFAR model. Third, the quantization pipeline is based on simulated PTQ with fake quantization, rather than a real INT4 inference implementation. Fourth, no real INT4 hardware latency, throughput, or energy measurement is reported. Finally, no quantization-aware training is considered, so the conclusions are restricted to post-training calibration behavior.

Future work will extend the evaluation to more compact CNN architectures such as MobileNetV2, where activation distributions and layer sensitivity may differ from ResNet-18-CIFAR. Another direction is to study mixed-precision quantization, assigning different bit-widths to layers according to sensitivity or deployment constraints. Hardware-aware quantization should also be investigated so that clipping and bit-width decisions can account for actual accelerator cost models. Finally, deploying the quantized models on an RFSoC platform would allow future work to connect the simulated PTQ results with real edge-hardware latency, resource utilization, and energy measurements.
